\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% =============================================================================

Understanding language is one of the hardest challenges in artificial
intelligence. Even humans rely on enormous amounts of world knowledge and
contextual reasoning to interpret a single sentence. For machines, the
challenge begins at the most basic unit of language: the word.

A central difficulty is \emph{polysemy} --- the fact that most words carry
multiple possible meanings, and the correct meaning is determined entirely by
context. Consider the sentence:

\begin{center}
  \textit{``I went to the \textbf{bank} to deposit money, then sat by the
  river \textbf{bank}.''}
\end{center}

\noindent
The word \textit{bank} appears twice, yet refers to entirely different
real-world entities. Any NLP system --- a search engine, a chatbot, or a
machine translator --- must resolve this ambiguity correctly to produce a
useful output.

This report traces the progression of ideas that led researchers to \bert{},
examining why each intermediate approach fell short and what design choices in
\bert{} finally addressed the core problem.
Section~\ref{sec:static} covers static word embeddings.
Section~\ref{sec:lstm} covers LSTMs and BiLSTMs.
Section~\ref{sec:transformer} introduces the Transformer.
Sections~\ref{sec:intro_bert} and~\ref{sec:turning_point} present \bert{} itself
and its significance. Section~\ref{sec:arch} provides a high-level architecture
overview.
